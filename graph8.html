Change in Profit

Henry Wu

The line graph compares the change in profit of various time of artifacts from 2000 to 2017. Overall, there were significant fluctuations in the profits for all Chinese artifacts.

In terms of ancient artifacts, the percentage change in profits varied the most. It peaked at over 80% in 2004 and reached a bottom of under -40% in 2006. On the other hand, artifacts from the late Qing Dynasty and from the modern showed a similar trend. Both of them reached a peak of around 60% in 2004 and went to a minimum of -40% and -20% respectively in 2006. Interestingly, between 2008 and 2011, there was a steady rise in profit but after that, the profits remained low.

The second line graph gives information about the profit changes for different prices of Chinese artifacts. Clearly, the patterns were extremely similar but the artifact with a higher price can earn a significant amount of money occasionally.

A closer look at the graph shows that the profit for the top 25% of the artifacts reached a peak of over 300% between 2003 and 2004. Similarly, the bottom 25% also reached the highest point at over 100% during the same period. Between 2008 and 2011, the profits grew gradually for all artifacts and after 2011, there were no big changes in profit.

The last graph shows the change in profit for artifacts sold in different locations, including Beijing, Hongkong, Newyork & London, and globally. In general, Beijing, Hong Kong, and Global artifacts had the strongest correlation.

Shockingly, the profit was very high in 2003 and 2005 for artifacts sold in Newyork or London (around 600% and over 500% respectively). In comparison, although there was a rise in profit for other locations during the same period, it was less significant. Between 2008 and 2011, the graph shows an increase in profits, even reaching up to 100% for Hong Kong, Newyork, and London. After that, the change in profit remained relatively stable.
